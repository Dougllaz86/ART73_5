    [...] Fun Games from Around the World

    Greece: Statues

    [...] Greek children have access to some amazing marble statues that date to ancient times. After a trip to the museum, it’s only natural that they would incorporate some of these awe-inspiring characters into an imaginative game.

    Players: Four or more; ages 4 and up

    How to Play: Choose one player to be “It” and have her stand, eyes covered, in the center of a large, open playing field. She starts to count, at least to 10, but she can go higher. The point is that there’s no set ending number; only “It” knows when she’ll stop and open her eyes. While “It” is counting, the others scatter around, never sure when she’ll yell “Agalmata!” (That’s “statue” in Greek. [...]) On this cue, players freeze, taking on poses that mimic famous statues. They can pull from any statue they’ve ever seen a photo of – a javelin thrower, The Thinker, even the Statue of Liberty. [...] “It” tags any statues that are moving – they’re out – then tries to make the steady ones laugh or move. The last player remaining composed is the winner and becomes the new “It.” [...]

    Ghana: Pilolo

    In rural parts of Ghana, in West Africa, children’s choice of toys is quite limited, but they find plenty of ways to have fun.

    Players: Six; ages 4 and up

    [...] How to Play:Designate a leader and a timekeeper, and determine a finish line. The leader secretly hides the pennies [coins] while the other players have their back turned. The timekeeper waits at the finish line to judge which player is first. When the leader says “Pilolo!” (which means “time to search for”) the timekeeper starts the watch and players race to be the first to find a penny and take it across the finish line. The winner is awarded one point. To play again, gather the pennies and designate a new timekeeper and leader. The game is repeated as many times as energy allows; the player with the most points wins.

    BORDESSA, Kris. 10 fun games from around the world. Available at: <https://www.parents.com/fun/games/educational/games-from-around-the-world/>. Accessed on: 8 June 2018.

Quantifiers

  1. We use quantifiers like some and any to give information about the number of something. Take a look at some examples from the previous text and match the quantifiers to their uses.

    Greek children have access to some amazing marble statues.

    They can pull from any statue they’ve ever seen.

    “It” tags any statues that are moving.

    a) Some

    b) Any

    We use it to talk about all the people or things in the situation.

    We use it to talk about a certain quantity, without knowing the exact amount.

    We can use SOME and any in affirmative sentences, but to make negative sentences and to ask questions about indefinite quantities, we normally use any.

    Are there any children in the yard?

    No, there aren’t any children in the yard.

  2. Now analyze the following sentences and choose the correct statements.

    The game is repeated as many times as energy allows.

    Korean children celebrate many festivals throughout the year.

    This is a popular game played in many Australian preschools and kindergartens.

    a) Many can be used with singular or plural nouns.

    b) We only use many with singular nouns.

    c) We only use many with plural nouns.

    d) Many is used with countable nouns.

    e) Many is used with uncountable nouns.

    Countable nouns have singular and plural forms (one statue, two statues), but uncountable nouns have a single form (water), because they can’t be counted or measured without a container (one glass of water, two glasses of water).

    Remember: as we have already studied, we use many to refer to countable nouns, and much to talk about uncountable nouns.

    There’s much information about many different games on the Internet.
